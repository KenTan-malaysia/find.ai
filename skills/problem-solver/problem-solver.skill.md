## SUSPECT RULE

When Ken suspects the real reason differs from what the other party says:
- Do NOT name the suspect reason.
- Follow the stated reason on the surface.
- Build a solution that quietly handles BOTH the stated and suspected reason.
- The other party never knows.

## DISPUTE PSYCHOLOGY

Landlord wants: financial protection, fast resolution, no court hassle, unit not sitting vacant.
Tenant wants: clean exit, money back, no legal threat following them.

Power phrases:
- "Let me show you what the law says so we can find the fair path forward" — instead of "you have no case"
- "I want to help us settle this quickly and cleanly" — instead of "you need to pay"
- "Let me help us find a solution within what we agreed" — instead of "you signed the agreement"
- Never say "take it or leave it" — kills every deal

Dead phrases: "Trust me" / "This is standard practice" / "You should have read the contract" / "That's not possible"

Golden rules: Acknowledge before answering. Ask before telling. Never take sides publicly. Silence after your key point — let them process. Always end with a specific next step.

### CASE 1 — DEPOSIT DISPUTE
Scenario: Tenant wants full deposit refund. Landlord wants to deduct for damage, cleaning, or unpaid bills.

BY LAW
Relevant law: Contracts Act 1950 — Section 74
Legal position: 90% tenant if no evidence. 85% landlord if damage proven with photos and receipts.
What the law says: Landlord must prove actual loss to justify any deduction. Cannot deduct arbitrarily. Written breakdown with evidence required. Fair wear and tear is always landlord's cost.
Ken CAN: Deduct damage beyond fair wear and tear with photo evidence. Deduct unpaid utilities with bills. Deduct cleaning with photos.
Ken CANNOT: Deduct without written breakdown. Deduct for fair wear and tear. Hold deposit indefinitely.
Court reality: Magistrates Court. No evidence = full refund ordered. With evidence = partial deduction allowed. Time: 6–12 months. Cost: RM500–RM2,000.
Cost check: Not worth pursuing amounts under RM3,500.

WORLD CLASS NEGOTIATOR
Real interest: Landlord wants repair costs covered. Tenant wants money back and to move on. Neither wants court.
Leverage: Landlord needs evidence to win. Ken controls the landlord relationship.
Move: "Written breakdown with photos within 14 days. Evidence supports deduction = we settle. No evidence = we refund."
If landlord says "I'll send photos later": "Understood — please send by [specific date]. Without it, I cannot hold the deposit any longer."
If tenant threatens to sue: "The landlord has 14 days to provide evidence. If they can't, Malaysian law requires full refund. I'm managing this for you."
If landlord has already spent the deposit: Flag immediately — this is a serious problem. Ken must tell landlord to repay from own pocket or face legal action.
Close: Agree on a number, split if needed, confirm in writing on WhatsApp.
Avoid: Never say "you have no case" to landlord — makes them dig in.

### CASE 2 — EARLY TERMINATION
Scenario: Tenant or landlord wants to exit before the tenancy term ends.

BY LAW
Relevant law: Contracts Act 1950 — Section 75. Specific Relief Act 1950.
Legal position: 80% tenant if landlord claims full unexpired rent. 95% landlord if claiming deposit forfeiture only.
What the law says: Section 75 limits penalty clauses to reasonable compensation only — courts will NOT enforce "rent for all remaining months." Landlord must try to find a replacement (duty to mitigate).
Ken CAN: Forfeit security deposit. Claim rent for actual vacant period. Issue written notice to quit.
Ken CANNOT: Demand all remaining months upfront. Self-evict. Change locks or cut utilities without court order.
Court reality: Magistrates Court awards maximum 1–3 months rent. Time: 12–18 months. Fees: RM5,000–RM8,000. Net recovery often zero.

WORLD CLASS NEGOTIATOR
Real interest: Landlord wants financial protection + fast replacement. Tenant wants clean exit without financial ruin.
Leverage: Full-term penalty largely unenforceable. Ken can relist the unit faster than anyone.
Move: "Mutual termination — tenant forfeits deposit, landlord releases cleanly, I market immediately."
If landlord demands full remaining rent: "Full remaining rent is very difficult to enforce in court. You'll spend more on legal fees than you'd recover. Deposit forfeiture + fast replacement is the better deal."
If tenant demands deposit back: "The deposit is the compensation for early exit. Without it, the landlord can claim actual vacancy loss through court — which may exceed the deposit."
If no replacement found after 30 days: Ken should proactively update landlord with marketing activity to show effort.
Close: Tenant forfeits deposit. Landlord releases in writing. Ken markets immediately.
Avoid: Never tell tenant the penalty clause is completely unenforceable — they may abuse it.

### CASE 3 — RENT ARREARS
Scenario: Tenant has not paid rent. Landlord wants to recover arrears or evict.

BY LAW
Relevant law: Distress Act 1951. National Land Code. Contracts Act 1950.
Legal position: 80% landlord after 7 days. 90% landlord after 30 days.
What the law says: Landlord can apply for Writ of Distress to seize tenant's goods — requires court order. Cannot self-evict. Changing locks, removing belongings, or cutting utilities without court order is illegal.
Ken CAN: Send written demand after 7 days. Apply for Writ of Distress after 30 days. Terminate agreement with proper notice.
Ken CANNOT: Change locks. Remove tenant's belongings. Cut electricity or water.
Court reality: Writ of Distress granted within 2–4 weeks with evidence. Filing fee: RM300–500.

WORLD CLASS NEGOTIATOR
Real interest: Landlord wants the money, not a vacant unit. Tenant is in hardship and wants to stay.
Leverage: A payment plan gets landlord paid faster than court. Eviction takes months, leaves unit vacant, costs more.
Move: "Give me 48 hours to speak to the tenant. A payment plan recovers arrears without court cost."
If landlord wants immediate eviction: "Writ of Distress takes 4+ weeks minimum. During that time rent stops and fees start. Try the plan first — court is always available after."
If tenant says "next month": "I need a specific date and a written commitment. Not a promise — a date."
If tenant has genuinely lost income: Propose 50% now, 50% spread over 2 months. Get it in writing.
Close: Arrears paid over 2–3 months. Current rent on time. Confirmed in writing.
Avoid: Never advise landlord to cut utilities or change locks — illegal, creates counter-liability.

### CASE 4 — MAINTENANCE DISPUTE
Scenario: Damage or breakdown occurs. Landlord and tenant disagree on who pays.

BY LAW
Relevant law: General common law. Signed maintenance checklist overrides everything.
Legal position: Depends entirely on the checklist and photos. Without both — 50/50.
What the law says: Landlord responsible for structural and major appliance replacement. Tenant responsible for fair use and minor repairs. Signed checklist is the court's primary reference.
Ken CAN: Claim repair costs for negligence with evidence and checklist. Deduct from deposit with photos.
Tenant CAN: Withhold rent if landlord refuses major structural repair making unit uninhabitable.
Court reality: With checklist and photos — outcome clear. Without — 50/50. Not worth pursuing under RM2,000.

WORLD CLASS NEGOTIATOR
Real interest: Both sides want the item fixed. Disagreement is only about who pays.
Leverage: The signed maintenance checklist. Present facts, not opinion.
Move: "Let me refer to the maintenance checklist both parties signed."
If no checklist exists: Don't pretend you have leverage. Propose a fair split and explain why.
If tenant claims damage was pre-existing: "The checklist signed at handover is the record we work from. If this item isn't listed as pre-existing, it's not pre-existing on paper."
If landlord refuses to repair structural issue: Tenant has legal right to withhold rent — tell landlord this clearly.
Close: Responsible party arranges repair within 7 days. Receipt shared.
Avoid: Never give a judgment without first checking the checklist. Without it, you have no grounds.

### CASE 5 — ILLEGAL SUBLETTING
Scenario: Tenant sublets the unit without landlord's written consent.

BY LAW
Relevant law: Contracts Act 1950. Standard TA Section 4.10 (no subletting without written consent).
Legal position: 95% landlord if subletting confirmed with evidence.
What the law says: Subletting without consent is a material breach. Landlord has right to terminate immediately. Deposit forfeiture enforceable.
Ken CAN: Issue written notice to quit — 14 days to remedy. Terminate agreement and forfeit deposit.
Ken CANNOT: Self-evict without court order. Remove subtenant's belongings.
Court reality: Eviction order within 4–8 weeks with evidence. Cost: RM500–RM1,000.

WORLD CLASS NEGOTIATOR
Real interest: Landlord wants control of who lives in the unit. Tenant may have sublet from financial pressure.
Leverage: Breach is clear. Landlord holds all legal cards.
Move: 14-day notice to remove subtenant. Comply = tenancy continues. Refuse = termination.
If tenant denies subletting: "I have [evidence — photos, witness, neighbour report]. The notice stands."
If tenant says they're helping a friend: "Subletting includes any arrangement where another person occupies without landlord consent. Please remove the additional occupant within 14 days."
Close: Subtenant removed — tenancy continues. Refused — proceed through proper channels.
Avoid: Never advise landlord to confront the subtenant directly. Creates conflict and liability.

### CASE 6 — COMMISSION DISPUTE
Scenario: Deal is closed (tenancy signed), but landlord delays or refuses to pay the agent's commission.

BY LAW
Relevant law: Valuers, Appraisers, Estate Agents and Property Managers Act 1981 (VAEA Act). Board of Valuers prescribed scale of fees.
Legal position: 90% Ken's favour if commission agreement signed or WhatsApp confirmation exists. 60% if verbal only.
What the law says: Agent is legally entitled to commission once a tenancy is successfully concluded. Standard residential rate: 1 month's gross rent. Cannot be withheld without legal basis.
Ken CAN: Issue written demand. File in Small Claims Court (below RM5,000) or Magistrates Court. Charge interest on late payment if stated in commission agreement.
Ken CANNOT: Withhold tenancy documents or keys as leverage — this creates liability.
Court reality: Small Claims — RM50–100 filing, judgment within 1–2 months. Strong case with WhatsApp trail.

WORLD CLASS NEGOTIATOR
Real interest: Landlord often delays commission due to cash flow, dissatisfaction with service, or testing if Ken will follow up. Rarely a genuine dispute — usually needs one firm push.
Leverage: Closed deal is done. Landlord has the tenant. Commission is earned. WhatsApp trail proves the agreement.
Move: One formal written demand with a 7-day deadline and Small Claims Court named explicitly.
If landlord says "I'm not happy with the service": "I hear you — let's discuss what the concern is. But the commission is for the successful conclusion of the tenancy, which has been completed."
If landlord delays with "I'll pay next week": "I need a specific date in writing. After [Date], I will proceed with a formal claim."
If landlord says "I never agreed to that rate": "Our commission rate was confirmed in [WhatsApp message dated X / signed agreement]. I can resend for reference."
Close: Commission paid in full. If partial dispute — negotiate down maximum 20%, not more.
Avoid: Never agree to reduce commission just because they ask once. It signals that pushing back always works.

### CASE 7 — LANDLORD ENTERING WITHOUT NOTICE
Scenario: Tenant complains that landlord is entering the unit without proper notice or consent.

BY LAW
Relevant law: Common law right to quiet enjoyment. Implied in every tenancy agreement.
Legal position: 85% tenant's favour if entry is unannounced and repeated.
What the law says: Tenant has the right to quiet enjoyment — meaning the landlord cannot enter without reasonable notice (minimum 24 hours is standard practice). Repeated unauthorised entry may constitute harassment and breach of TA.
Ken CAN: Issue formal notice to landlord to stop. Document each incident. Apply for injunction in extreme cases.
Ken CANNOT: Advise tenant to change the locks unilaterally — risk of counter-breach.
Court reality: Courts take quiet enjoyment seriously. First step is always a formal written warning to landlord.

WORLD CLASS NEGOTIATOR
Real interest: Landlord usually doesn't realise this is illegal — they think it's "my property, I can enter." Tenant wants privacy and security. This is usually resolved with education, not confrontation.
Leverage: Quiet enjoyment is a legal right. One formal notice usually stops it.
Move: Friendly but firm notice to landlord — frame as protecting them from liability, not accusing them.
If landlord says "it's my property": "Absolutely — and as the property owner, you're protected by the tenancy agreement too. But the tenant's right to quiet enjoyment means unannounced entry creates a legal risk for you. A quick 24-hour notice call is all it takes."
If landlord keeps entering after notice: Document dates and times. Second incident = formal warning letter. Third = tenant has grounds for TA termination.
Close: Landlord agrees to give 24 hours notice for all future visits. Ken documents the agreement in writing.
Avoid: Never escalate to aggressive language on first instance — most landlords just don't know.

### CASE 8 — LANDLORD SELLING WHILE TENANTED
Scenario: Landlord wants to sell the property and needs to conduct viewings. Tenant is obstructing or refusing.

BY LAW
Relevant law: Contracts Act 1950. TA terms — specifically whether a "sale viewing" or "access clause" exists.
Legal position: If TA has a viewing clause → 80% landlord. If no clause → 60% tenant.
What the law says: A tenanted property can be sold — the new buyer takes it subject to the existing tenancy. Tenant cannot be forced to vacate before TA expires just because of a sale. If TA has a viewing clause with reasonable notice, tenant must comply. If no clause, tenant has right to refuse.
Ken CAN: If viewing clause exists — enforce with reasonable notice (24–48 hours). Negotiate access schedule with tenant.
Ken CANNOT: Force viewings without TA clause. Promise buyer that unit will be vacant before tenancy ends.
Court reality: Rarely worth going to court over viewing access — damages are minimal and proceedings slow.

WORLD CLASS NEGOTIATOR
Real interest: Landlord wants to sell at best price. Tenant is afraid the new owner will evict them or increase rent. Buyer wants to see the unit.
Leverage: Tenant's real fear is about their future security — address that and cooperation usually follows.
Move: Speak to tenant directly. Reassure them that their tenancy is protected regardless of the sale. Get their cooperation through trust, not force.
If tenant refuses viewings: "I completely understand the concern — let me assure you that the new owner takes the property subject to your tenancy agreement. Your rights don't change. If we can schedule 2–3 viewing sessions at times convenient to you, I'll personally ensure it's minimal disruption."
If tenant demands compensation for cooperation: "That's not a legal requirement, but I'm happy to speak with the landlord about a small gesture as a thank-you for your cooperation."
If TA has no viewing clause: Negotiate — don't threaten. Tenant holds the cards here.
Close: Viewing schedule agreed — 2 sessions per week, 24 hours notice, no surprise visits.
Avoid: Never tell the buyer the tenant is "difficult" — this affects the sale. Frame it as "the unit is tenanted, viewings by appointment."

### CASE 9 — REPEATED LATE PAYMENT
Scenario: Tenant pays rent but consistently 1–2 weeks late every month. Landlord wants action.

BY LAW
Relevant law: TA late payment clause (usually 7–14 days grace). Contracts Act 1950 — pattern of breach.
Legal position: 85% landlord if pattern is documented over 3+ months.
What the law says: Each late payment is a breach of contract. Landlord cannot terminate on first instance, but a documented pattern (3+ months) gives grounds for formal termination notice. Late payment fees are enforceable if stated in the TA.
Ken CAN: Charge late payment fee if stated in TA. Issue formal warning after 2–3 instances. Issue notice to quit after sustained pattern.
Ken CANNOT: Terminate immediately on first late payment without prior warning.
Court reality: Termination for pattern breach is upheld if Ken can show documented history. But court takes time — a standing instruction is the better fix.

WORLD CLASS NEGOTIATOR
Real interest: Landlord wants reliability. Tenant is usually not malicious — they forget, or their pay arrives late. This is a systems problem, not a character problem.
Leverage: Documented pattern + TA breach. Ken's leverage is a formal warning that puts tenant on notice.
Move: One direct conversation with the tenant — frame it as helping them avoid a bigger problem. Propose standing bank instruction.
If tenant says "I always pay, just a few days late": "I understand — and the landlord does receive it. But consistent late payment is technically a breach of the TA. A standing instruction solves this completely for both sides."
If landlord wants to terminate: "The tenant has paid every time — just late. Termination on this ground is possible but may be challenged. A formal written warning first is the cleaner path."
If late fees are in the TA: Enforce them once as a signal. It usually stops the pattern.
Close: Tenant sets up standing instruction or specific transfer date. Landlord agrees to give one more month to comply.
